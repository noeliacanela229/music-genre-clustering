The Project

What We Set Out to Do

We used a Spotify dataset of 32,000+ songs and tried to see if unsupervised clustering algorithms could discover genre groupings just from audio features — things like tempo, energy, danceability, and loudness — without ever being told what genres exist.

We ran 6 different algorithms and compared them using silhouette score to find out which approach actually works best for music data.

Methods

Algorithms We Ran

We ran six clustering algorithms to compare which one groups music genres most meaningfully.

Baseline

K-Means

Standard clustering — assigns each song to its nearest cluster center. Used as our baseline for comparison.

Best Score

K-Means++

Same as K-Means but with smarter initialization — spreads starting points out to get cleaner clusters.

Robust

K-Medoids

Uses actual songs as cluster centers instead of averages — more stable when there are outliers.

Density-Based

DBSCAN

Groups songs by density rather than distance. Flags songs that don't fit anywhere as noise.

Soft Assignments

GMM

Gives each song a probability of belonging to each cluster instead of a hard assignment.

Tree Structure

Hierarchical

Builds a tree showing how songs and genres relate and merge together at different levels.

Key Findings

Results

🏆

K-Means Won overall

K-Means and K-Means++ tied at 0.1384 — simple distance-based clustering worked best for this type of data.

⚡

DBSCAN Failed

Found 46 clusters and labeled over half the songs as noise. Music genres blend into each other too much for density-based methods.

🔗

Features Overlap

Energy and loudness are strongly correlated at 0.68 — which explains why genres like EDM and rock end up in similar clusters.

🎵

Cluster 5 Stood Out

One cluster had an average tempo of 169 BPM — noticeably faster than all others which averaged around 110–125.
